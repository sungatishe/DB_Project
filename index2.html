General Information

Presumably, long-period comets arrive in the inner solar system from the Oort cloud, which contains a huge number of cometary nuclei. Bodies located on the outskirts of the solar system, as a rule, consist of volatile substances (water, methane and other gases) that evaporate when approaching the Sun. More than 400 short-period comets have been discovered so far[2]. Of these, about 200 have been observed in more than one perihelion passage. Many of them are included in the so-called families.The brightness of comets depends very much on their distance from the Sun. Of all the comets, only a very small part approaches the Sun and the Earth enough to be seen with the naked eye. The most notable ones are sometimes referred to as "large (great) comets". Many of the meteors we observe (â€œshooting starsâ€) are of cometary origin. These are particles lost by a comet that burn up when they enter the atmosphere of planets.

Nomenclature

Over the past centuries, the rules for naming comets have often changed and been refined. Until the beginning of the 20th century, most comets were called for their discovery, sometimes with an increase in the frequency of observations of the relative increase or year, if there were several comets in that year. For example, "The Great January Comet of 1680", "The Great September Comet of 1882", "The Daytime Comet of 1910" ("The Great January Comet of 1910"). After Halley discovered that the comets of 1531, 1607, and 1682 were the same comet and predicted its return in 1759, the comet's occultation became known as Halley's Comet.
