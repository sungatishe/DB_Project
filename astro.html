Astronauts

Astronaut

An Astronaut is a person who tests and operates space technology in space flight. The concept of space flight in different countries is different. According to the classification of the International Federation of Aeronautics (FAI), a flight is considered to be space flight, the height of which exceeds 100 km. According to the classification of the US Air Force, a flight in space is considered to be a flight whose altitude exceeds 50 miles (80 km 467 m). In Russia, space flight is called orbital flight, that is, a flight in which the device must make at least one revolution around the Earth. That is why the total number of astronauts in different sources varies.

the first Astronaut in the world

Gagarin, Yuri Alekseevich

Yuri Alekseevich Gagarin (March 9, 1934, Klushino, Gzhatsky (now Gagarinsky) district, Western region (now Smolensk region) - March 27, 1968, near the village of Novosyolovo, Kirzhachsky district, Vladimir region) - USSR pilot-cosmonaut, Hero of the Soviet Union, cavalier of the highest distinctions of a number of states, honorary citizen of many Russian and foreign cities On April 12, 1961, Yuri Gagarin became the first person in world history to fly into outer space[4][5]. The Vostok launch vehicle with the Vostok-1 spacecraft carrying Gagarin was launched from the Baikonur cosmodrome in the Kyzylorda region of Kazakhstan. After 108 minutes [6] of the flight, Gagarin successfully landed in the Saratov region, not far from Engels. April 12, 1961, the day of Yuri Gagarin's flight into space, was declared a holiday - Cosmonautics Day. On March 27, 1968, Yuri Gagarin died in a plane crash near the village of Novoselovo, Kirzhachsky District, Vladimir Region, while performing a training flight on a MiG-15UTI aircraft under the guidance of an experienced instructor V.S. Seregin. The causes and circumstances of the plane crash remain unclear until today.

Famous Astronauts

Statistics

As of April 9, 2020, there are 564 people (500 men and 64 women) who have completed an orbital space flight (see the list of cosmonauts and astronauts participating in orbital space flights); 11 people[2][3][4][5] who made a suborbital space flight along a ballistic trajectory with a height of more than 100 km (according to the FAI classification); 9 people who made a suborbital space flight on a ballistic trajectory with an altitude of more than 50 miles (80.4672 km), but less than 100 km (according to the US Air Force classification).

Historical facts

April 12, 1961 - the first cosmonaut in history was Yuri Gagarin (USSR), who made a space flight on the Vostok spacecraft. May 5, 1961 - The first American astronaut was Alan Shepard, who made a suborbital flight on the Mercury 3 spacecraft. August 6, 1961 - the youngest cosmonaut was 25-year-old German Titov (USSR), who flew on the Vostok-2 spacecraft. The world's first space flight lasting more than a day. For the first time, an astronaut slept in zero gravity. February 20, 1962 - The first American astronaut, according to the FAI, was John Glenn, who flew on the Mercury 6 spacecraft. June 16, 1963 - Valentina Tereshkova (USSR) became the first female cosmonaut to fly on the Vostok-6 spacecraft.

International space

Until the end of the 1970s, only two countries - the USSR and the USA - had cosmonauts. In 1976, the Interkosmos program was launched in the Soviet Union, within the framework of which the first group, consisting of 6 cosmonauts from socialist countries, began training. In 1978, a second group was added to it. Around the same time, ESA selected 4 people who began training for the first flight of the Spacelab program aboard the Space Shuttle. In 1980, France independently began its own selection of astronauts, followed by Germany in 1982, Canada in 1983, Japan in 1985, and Italy in 1988.
